</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><span lang="en-us"><p>Assume you have a square of size <i>n</i> that is divided into <i>n &times; n</i> positions just as a checkerboard. Two positions (<i>x</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>y</i><sub>1</sub>) and (<i>x</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>y</i><sub>2</sub>), where 1 &le; <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>y</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>y</i><sub>2</sub> &le; <i>n</i>, are called &ldquo;independent&rdquo; if they occupy different rows and different columns, that is, <i>x</i><sub>1</sub> &ne; <i>x</i><sub>2</sub> and <i>y</i><sub>1</sub> &ne; <i>y</i><sub>2</sub>. More generally, <i>n</i> positions are called independent if they are pairwise independent. It follows that there are <i>n</i>! different ways to choose <i>n</i> independent positions. </p><p>Assume further that a number is written in each position of such an <i>n</i> &times; <i>n</i> square. This square is called &ldquo;homogeneous&rdquo; if the sum of the numbers written in <i>n</i> independent positions is the same, no matter how the positions are chosen.
